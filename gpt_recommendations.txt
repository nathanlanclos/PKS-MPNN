1) Don’t choose “full module” vs “domain” — choose design region vs structural context

ProteinMPNN is residue-graph-based: each residue “sees” a local 3D neighborhood (K nearest neighbors). So you can keep interface context without paying the full-noisy-module penalty by doing:

Feed ProteinMPNN a crop that contains the region you want to learn + all residues within a distance cutoff

Compute loss only on the region you want to predict

Keep the surrounding residues as fixed context (sequence provided / masked from loss)

This is the best of both worlds:

You preserve cross-domain contacts that matter for residue identity.

You avoid training on massive low-confidence flexible chunks.

Minimum viable thing (easy, high impact)

Mask loss to residues with pLDDT ≥ 70 (or 80 if you want to be strict early).

Or weight loss by confidence:

w_i = clamp((pLDDT_i - 50)/50, 0, 1) (simple linear)

If you can use PAE (even better)

Inter-domain orientation is often what’s unstable.

When building the graph edges or when cropping context, downweight / drop edges between residues whose mutual PAE is high (e.g., > 10–15 Å).

This prevents the model from learning “contacts” that are artifacts of uncertain domain placement.

5) Yes, cluster & deduplicate — but do it to protect evaluation, not to erase “structural variability”

With 1600 modules, overfitting + leakage are your real enemies. Clustering is mostly about getting a meaningful held-out test.

What to cluster on

I’d cluster on sequence, because:

Structure variability across AF3 models for the same sequence is uncertainty, not independent signal.

“True” structural diversity that matters for generalization is usually coupled to sequence divergence anyway.

Two good options:

Cluster on full module sequence (simple, strict)

Cluster on KS (or KS–AT) only if architectures vary wildly and you care about cross-architecture generalization

Thresholds (practical)

Use 70–80% identity clustering for splitting families (depends how conservative you want to be).

Then do train/val/test splits by cluster, not by individual proteins.

Also consider stratifying by architecture so your test set isn’t missing a whole class.

6) What to do about “training on full modules may preserve interfaces”

You’re right in principle, but the key nuance is:

ProteinMPNN needs correct local 3D neighborhoods.

If AF3 is confident in a domain but not in relative domain placement, full-module training can destroy the neighborhood signal with wrong contacts.

That’s why “crop-with-context + confidence/PAE-aware masking” is the sweet spot: you preserve real interfaces and ignore uncertain long-range junk.

When training on “wrong” AF3 geometry can still help

It can be beneficial if your downstream loop is self-consistent in AF3-space, e.g.:

You plan to score/evaluate designs with AF3 (or AF-family predictors)
Then distilling the AF3 manifold into your inverse model can make design much faster:

“Give me sequences that AF3 will predict as plausible for this scaffold.”

Even if the scaffold is not physically correct, the model can produce sequences that sit on the AF3-credible manifold.

You’re using AF3 as a compression medium / canonicalization tool
For giant, flexible proteins, the “true” structure is an ensemble. AF3 gives you a consistent representation that might correlate with function in a coarse way (e.g., KS–AT geometry), even if linkers/loops are off.

You only care about local geometry in confident regions
Even if global domain placement is wrong, the local neighborhoods inside high-confidence domains can still be quite informative for residue identity.

Why this can fail badly (the core danger)

The fact that the sequences are natural and functional does not guarantee that the particular AF3 geometry you’re conditioning on is causally related to the sequence constraints.

Two key failure modes:

1) You teach the inverse model to explain artifacts

If AF3 invents contacts, the inverse model will learn that certain residues are “required” to satisfy those contacts. That can:

reduce generalization,

push designs toward sequences that “please AF3” rather than sequences that fold/function.

This is exactly the classic problem of exploiting simulator bugs in model-based design: you optimize to a biased world model and get designs that don’t transfer.

2) Flexible regions aren’t single-structure-determined

In low-confidence regions (loops, linkers, swinging domains), sequence is shaped by an ensemble of conformations + interactions + dynamics, not one static pose. Conditioning on one random predicted pose can inject anti-signal: the model “must” fit a geometry that the protein doesn’t actually adopt reliably.

The middle ground that usually wins

If you want to use AF3 as the “medium” and keep designs biologically meaningful, treat AF3 geometry as a noisy observation with uncertainty, not ground truth.

Practically, that means:

Ensemble training, not single-structure training

Use all 5 AF3 models per module as augmentations.

But train the inverse model to be robust to that variability, not to memorize one pose.

Confidence-aware learning

Mask or downweight loss where geometry is likely nonsense (low pLDDT / high PAE / high ensemble spread).

This still lets you leverage AF3 as a medium, but you’re explicitly telling the model: “don’t learn hard constraints from unreliable geometry.”

Context-preserving crops

Keep local neighborhoods and real interfaces when they’re confident.

Avoid forcing the model to condition on a full-module global arrangement that AF3 can’t reliably nail.

A crisp decision rule

If your goal is AF3-consistent design (you’ll choose candidates largely based on AF3 scores), then training on AF3 geometry can absolutely be strategic — it’s distillation.

If your goal is real-world functional design (wet lab, activity, stability), then uncritical training on “nonsense” geometry invites a simulator-exploitation problem. You’ll likely do better with confidence-aware training + ensemble robustness.

A simple experiment to settle it empirically

Train two variants and compare where they succeed:

Naïve AF3-inversion model: full module, no masking

Uncertainty-aware model: ensemble sampling + confidence/PAE masking + crops

Then evaluate designs in three ways:

AF3 self-consistency: does AF3 predict high confidence for sequences sampled from the model?

Cross-predictor consistency: do AF2/other predictors agree (even roughly) on the fold/domain packing?

Motif/constraint sanity: catalytic motifs, domain signatures, conserved active-site patterns remain intact.

If (1) looks great on AF3 but collapses on cross-predictors / motif sanity, you’ve directly observed simulator overfitting. If (2) is strong across all three, you’ve got a design model that uses AF3 as a medium without becoming enslaved to its artifacts.
